Why inverse-square-like decay in Prediction One.
My model claims spacetime emerges from entanglement. It states that the entanglement entropy between two regions is proportional to the area of the minimal surface separating them, divided by four times the Planck area. In a three-dimensional emergent space, the minimal surface bounding a region at distance r is a two-dimensional sphere with area proportional to r squared. Therefore, if I sever entanglement and the effective distance r increases, the residual mutual information — which was encoding proximity — must decrease in proportion to one over the area of the new minimal surface. One over r squared. Inverse square.
But I must be precise about what I mean. I am not claiming the mutual information transfer rate falls off as one over r squared in the qubit system's native Hilbert space. I am claiming that if I define an effective emergent distance d from the mutual information using the Ryu-Takayanagi relation, and then I plot the transfer rate as a function of d, the relationship will follow the same geodesic falloff that characterizes signal propagation in a smooth three-dimensional space — which is inverse square for a scalar quantity radiating isotropically.
Why inverse square specifically and not some other power law? Because the emergent space I am predicting is three-dimensional. In a d-dimensional emergent space, the falloff would go as one over r to the power d minus one. A 2D boundary encodes a 3D bulk. Three spatial dimensions gives d minus one equals two, which gives inverse square.
If the experiment shows a decay that goes as one over r to the power 1.5 or one over r cubed, that tells me the emergent dimensionality is not three, and either my assumption about the boundary-to-bulk dimensional relationship is wrong, or the specific entanglement pattern I prescribed does not produce a clean three-dimensional bulk geometry. Both are possible failure modes.
The reasoning chain: Ryu-Takayanagi links entanglement to geometry. Geometry in three dimensions gives spherical minimal surfaces. Spherical surfaces have area proportional to r squared. Why 3.2 percent in Prediction Two.
This is where I must be most honest, because this number required the most assumptions.
I start from the Crooks fluctuation theorem, which gives the probability ratio of entropy-decreasing to entropy-increasing trajectories in a small system. For a system of n particles, the standard prediction is that the probability of observing a spontaneous entropy decrease of magnitude delta S is proportional to e to the negative delta S over k Boltzmann. This is well established and I derived it independently from the statistical mechanics framework I built using the lottery database and power plant data.

Step one. My model says boundaries carry real physical information. The boundary of the optical tweezer trapping potential is not just a mathematical convenience — it is a surface on which information about the system's relationship to the external vacuum is encoded.

Step three. I apply the Bekenstein bound, which limits the information content of a region based on its boundary area. At 50 nanokelvin, the thermal de Broglie wavelength of rubidium is approximately 1.2 micrometers. Step four. Step five. For 15 rubidium atoms at 50 nanokelvin in a harmonic trap, the number of accessible internal microstates can be estimated from the partition function. Step six. The correction is log of 16.7 divided by 520. Log of 16.7 is approximately 2.8. The ratio is 2.8 divided by 520, which is 0.0054. But this correction applies to the exponent in the fluctuation theorem, and the observable is the rate, which involves the exponential of the corrected exponent. For small corrections to the exponent, the fractional change in the rate is approximately equal to the average delta S times the correction factor. For the most probable observable entropy fluctuations in a 15-atom system, average delta S over k is approximately 6. So the fractional rate change is 6 times 0.0054, which is 0.032.

I will now tell you every place this derivation could be wrong. The thermal de Broglie wavelength calculation assumes a non-interacting gas, which rubidium at 50 nanokelvin is not — at these temperatures, Bose-Einstein correlations become significant and the effective wavelength changes. My estimate of the trap geometry is generic, not specific to any particular experimental setup. The assumption that boundary cells contribute independent microstates is the core claim of my model and is precisely what the experiment is designed to test. The linearization in step six is valid only if the correction is small, which it is, but the specific value of average delta S depends on the observation protocol.

If it returns a correction but at a different value — say 5 percent or 1 percent — then the mechanism may be real but my specific calculation of boundary cell count or phase space volume is off, and I would revise the geometric assumptions before abandoning the framework.

I stop here and admit something.
The 0.8 percent figure cannot be derived purely from established constants using my framework without making an additional assumption that I have not yet justified.
