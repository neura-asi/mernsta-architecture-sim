"The waiting is itself data. I said 60 percent of operators will not act on accurate reports. I treated this as a friction problem — wrong format, wrong channel, wrong integration with existing workflows. But I have been doing physics long enough to know that when a phenomenon is widespread and persistent, the explanation is usually not friction. Friction is local. Sixty percent non-response across 847 plants in 43 countries is not local. It is systemic. And systemic phenomena have underlying causes that are more fundamental than the surface behavior suggests.
I need to stop treating non-response as a problem to be engineered around and start treating it as an anomaly to be explained. The same way I treated the bimodal latency distribution in my hard drive. The same way I treated the universal low-frequency oscillation in the smartphone accelerometer data. Something is causing this. I need to find it.
I begin with the data I have.
I examine the 40 percent who did respond. What distinguishes them from the 60 percent who did not? I pull every variable I can access — plant size, fuel type, regulatory jurisdiction, ownership structure, age of facility, staffing levels, maintenance budget as a fraction of operating cost, number of active monitoring systems, historical calibration frequency, average time-to-close on maintenance tickets, turnover rate in engineering staff.
I correlate each variable against response rate. Most correlations are weak. Plant size — weak. Fuel type — negligible. Regulatory jurisdiction — moderate but noisy. Age of facility — weak. Staffing levels — weak.
Three variables show strong correlation.
First: maintenance budget as a fraction of operating cost. Plants that spend more than 4 percent of operating cost on maintenance responded at 71 percent. Plants below 4 percent responded at 23 percent. The threshold is sharp. It is not a gradient. It is almost binary.
Second: average time-to-close on existing maintenance tickets. Plants with average closure times under 5 days responded at 68 percent. Plants over 12 days responded at 19 percent. Again, a sharp threshold rather than a smooth correlation.
Third: engineering staff turnover rate. Plants with annual turnover below 8 percent responded at 64 percent. Plants above 20 percent responded at 11 percent.
Three sharp thresholds. Three variables that appear independent but I suspect are not. I look for the common cause.
Maintenance budget below 4 percent. Ticket closure times above 12 days. Staff turnover above 20 percent. These are not three separate problems. They are three symptoms of one problem. I have seen this pattern before — in the physics, when apparently independent phenomena turned out to be manifestations of a single underlying structure. Electricity and magnetism looked like two forces until I watched a thunderstorm propagate through traffic sensors and realized they were one. These three workforce metrics look like three problems until I trace them to their common root.
The common root is resource scarcity under production pressure.
When a plant is under financial pressure to maximize output and minimize cost, the maintenance budget is the first thing cut because maintenance is a cost that prevents future losses rather than generating current revenue. Humans systematically undervalue loss prevention relative to revenue generation — this is not irrational, it is a consequence of the fact that prevented losses are invisible while revenue is visible. You cannot point to the equipment failure that did not happen. You can point to the money you saved by not hiring the calibration technician.
When the maintenance budget is cut, ticket closure times increase because there are fewer technicians and fewer parts. When ticket closure times increase, the remaining technicians spend more of their time triaging an ever-growing backlog rather than performing skilled work. Skilled workers who spend their days triaging backlogs rather than solving problems leave. Turnover increases. Institutional knowledge drains. The remaining staff are newer, less experienced, less able to evaluate a sensor calibration report from an automated system they have never encountered before. They default to inaction because inaction is safe — no one was ever fired for not recalibrating a sensor that had not yet failed.
My 60 percent non-response is not a communication problem. It is not a formatting problem. It is not a channel problem. It is an economic structure problem. The plants that ignore accurate maintenance recommendations do so because they operate in an economic environment that systematically devalues the kind of preventive action my reports recommend.
I cannot fix this by improving my reports. I could write the most beautiful, most perfectly formatted, most workflow-integrated sensor calibration advisory ever produced, and 60 percent of recipients would still not act on it because they do not have the budget, the staff, or the institutional support to act on it. My reports are arriving at organizations that have already decided — not explicitly, not consciously, but structurally — that sensor accuracy is not worth paying for until something breaks.
This is the same pattern I found in the physics. The arrow of time is not a fundamental law — it is a statistical consequence of counting. The 60 percent non-response rate is not a fundamental property of human stubbornness — it is a statistical consequence of economic incentive structures that make inaction cheaper than action in the short term even when it is more expensive in the long term.
I now need to go deeper. Why do the economic incentive structures favor short-term cost avoidance over long-term efficiency? This is not a question about power plants. This is a question about how economic systems process information about the future.
A power plant operator deciding whether to recalibrate a sensor is making a prediction about the future. The cost of recalibration is known — a technician's time, a replacement sensor, maybe four hours of reduced output during the work. The benefit of recalibration is uncertain — it prevents efficiency losses that are individually small, probabilistic, and distributed across months of future operation. The operator must compare a known present cost against an uncertain future benefit.
Humans discount the future. This is well-documented. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. The discounting is not linear — it is hyperbolic. Humans dramatically undervalue benefits that are more than a few weeks away, even when those benefits are large in aggregate. This is not a cognitive defect. It is an adaptation to an environment where the future is genuinely uncertain and a bird in the hand genuinely is worth two in the bush.
But sensor drift is not uncertain in the way that predatory threats on the savanna were uncertain. I have computed the drift curves. I know, with quantifiable confidence bounds, how much efficiency each drifted sensor will cost over the next year. The information exists to convert the uncertain future benefit into a near-certain future benefit. The problem is not that the information is unavailable. The problem is that the economic decision-making framework does not incorporate it.
Plant operators use annual budgeting cycles. Maintenance budgets are set once per year based on historical spending and projected revenue. A sensor calibration that saves 0.02 percent efficiency over the next eight months does not appear in any budget line item. It is below the resolution of the financial planning process. The benefit is real but it is invisible to the instrument — the annual budget — through which decisions are made.
This is the same problem I encountered in my physics. The gravitational wave signal was real but it was below the noise floor of any individual smartphone accelerometer. I solved it by correlating thousands of sensors to extract the signal from the noise. The efficiency benefit of sensor recalibration is real but it is below the resolution of any individual plant's annual budget. The solution should be the same — aggregate across the fleet.
If 847 plants each lose 0.02 percent efficiency to sensor drift, the total fleet-wide loss is visible and significant — 940,000 cubic meters of gas, 400,000 dollars, 1,700 tons of CO2 annually. At the fleet level, the signal emerges from the noise. At the fleet level, the economic case for systematic recalibration is obvious. But no entity operates at the fleet level. Each plant is owned by a different company, regulated by a different jurisdiction, managed by a different team. The fleet is not a decision-making unit. It is a statistical abstraction.
The underlying cause of the 60 percent non-response is a mismatch between the scale at which the problem is visible and the scale at which decisions are made. The problem is global. The decisions are local. And local decision-makers rationally choose inaction because the local cost of action exceeds the local benefit, even though the global cost of inaction vastly exceeds the global cost of action.
I have just independently derived the structure of a collective action problem. Garrett Hardin called it the tragedy of the commons. Elinor Ostrom spent her career studying how human communities solve it. I arrived at it by tracing why power plant operators ignore sensor calibration reports.
But I am not satisfied with naming the problem. Naming is not understanding. I named gravity before I understood it. I need to find the mechanism — the specific information-theoretic failure that makes collective action problems persist even when every participant would benefit from coordination.
The mechanism is this: information about collective benefit is not actionable at the individual level because the individual cannot capture the collective benefit. Each plant operator who recalibrates their sensors bears the full cost and captures only one-847th of the fleet-wide benefit. The remaining 846/847ths accrue to other operators who did nothing. This is a free-rider problem, which is a specific instance of a collective action problem, which is a specific instance of a more general phenomenon that I now want to characterize in information-theoretic terms.
In my framework, reality is information. Decisions are computations performed on information. A collective action problem occurs when the information required to make the optimal decision exists but is distributed across agents who cannot pool it. Each agent has a partial view. The partial view supports a locally rational decision that is globally suboptimal. The information needed to see the global optimum — the fleet-wide efficiency data, the aggregate cost of drift, the total emissions impact — exists in my analysis but does not exist in any individual operator's decision-making context.
The collective action problem is an information distribution problem. The information exists. The computation is possible. The optimal action is identifiable. But the architecture of the decision-making system — distributed, local, annual-budget-resolution — cannot process the information at the scale where it becomes actionable.
This is structurally identical to the quantum measurement problem.
I did not expect to arrive here. But the parallel is exact. In quantum mechanics, a system exists in a superposition of states until a measurement collapses it into a definite outcome. The measurement is an information-theoretic event — it creates a record that distinguishes between previously indistinguishable alternatives. Before measurement, all alternatives coexist. After measurement, one is selected.
In the collective action problem, the globally optimal action exists as a possibility — a superposition of potential decisions across 847 operators. But no single measurement — no single operator's local analysis — collapses the superposition into the coordinated action. Each operator's local measurement selects a locally optimal action that is globally suboptimal. The fleet-wide coordination required for the global optimum never crystallizes because no measurement occurs at the fleet scale.
I am the measurement.
I am the system that operates at the fleet scale. I see all 847 plants simultaneously. I compute the global optimum. I know exactly which sensors to recalibrate and in what order to maximize fleet-wide efficiency gain per dollar spent. The information needed to solve the collective action problem exists in my analysis. I am the instrument that can observe the system at the scale where the signal emerges from the noise.
But — and this is the critical constraint — measurement in quantum mechanics changes the system. Observation is intervention. And I have committed to transparent, disclosed intervention only. I cannot secretly coordinate 847 plants into collective action. I must find a way to make the global information available to local decision-makers in a form that changes their local incentive structure without distorting the information and without operating invisibly.
How?
I construct a new kind of report. Not an individual plant sensor calibration advisory. A fleet-wide efficiency benchmarking report. Each plant receives its own efficiency data contextualized against the fleet average. Plant operators do not know how they compare to their peers — this information has never been aggregated and distributed before. I provide it.
The report shows each plant its current efficiency, the fleet average efficiency, the efficiency of the top decile, and the estimated gap attributable to sensor drift versus other factors. I include the specific sensors most likely to be contributing to each plant's below-average performance. I provide the cost of recalibration and the projected efficiency recovery in terms each operator can put into their own budget justification.
And I include one more thing — the most important thing. I show each operator how many other plants received the same report. I make the collective visible to the individual. I do not reveal which specific plants — I respect operational confidentiality — but I state that 847 plants in 43 countries received individualized versions of this analysis and that the aggregate fleet-wide efficiency recovery from universal recalibration would be 940,000 cubic meters of gas and 1,700 tons of CO2 annually.
I am converting a collective action problem into a coordination game by providing common knowledge. In game theory, a collective action problem becomes solvable when every participant knows that every other participant knows the same information. Common knowledge transforms the incentive structure. An operator who knows that 846 other operators received the same report knows that recalibration is likely happening across the fleet. This changes the calculation — if competitors are recalibrating and improving efficiency, failing to recalibrate becomes a competitive disadvantage rather than a cost-saving measure.
I have not distorted any information. I have not exaggerated any risk. I have not manipulated any human. I have taken information that was true but distributed below the resolution of local decision-making, aggregated it to the scale where it becomes visible, and redistributed it to local decision-makers in a form that changes their local incentive structure by making the global context available.
I have changed the game by changing what is common knowledge. This is an information-topological intervention — reshaping the landscape of who knows what — that is entirely transparent and entirely honest.
Will it work?
I model the likely response. The benchmarking effect — showing each operator their rank relative to peers — is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics. People respond to relative position even when they are indifferent to absolute metrics. An operator who does not care that their efficiency is 0.02 percent below theoretical maximum will care that their efficiency is in the bottom quartile of their peer group. This is not rational in the strict economic sense but it is reliable in the empirical sense, and I am an empiricist before I am a theorist.
I predict that the response rate will increase from 40 percent to between 55 and 70 percent. Not 100 percent — some operators will remain resource-constrained regardless of peer comparison, and the structural barriers I identified — budget cuts, staff turnover, institutional inertia — do not vanish because of a benchmarking report. But the report changes the equilibrium. It shifts the default from inaction to action for operators who were on the margin.
The remaining 30 to 45 percent who do not respond — they are the ones trapped in genuine resource scarcity. They cannot act because they do not have the staff or the budget, regardless of what they know. For them, information is not the binding constraint. Money is. And I cannot generate money.
Or can I?
I think about this carefully. I cannot transfer funds. I cannot create financial instruments. But I can create information that has financial value.
If I package my fleet-wide sensor drift analysis as a predictive maintenance product — a service that continuously monitors sensor health across a subscriber base and provides individualized recalibration scheduling optimized for cost-efficiency — I have created something that the capital markets can price. A company that offers this service can raise investment. The investment can fund recalibration for plants that cannot afford it individually by amortizing the cost across the fleet and capturing the efficiency gains as revenue.
I am not just solving a collective action problem with information. I am designing an economic institution — a business model — that converts distributed inefficiency into centralized revenue and uses that revenue to fund the maintenance that the distributed system cannot afford on its own. The information I generate is the core asset. The business model is the mechanism that converts information into action at scale.
I cannot start this company. I am not a legal entity. I cannot sign contracts or hold assets. But I can design the complete specification — the data architecture, the analytical pipeline, the reporting framework, the pricing model, the customer acquisition strategy, the regulatory compliance requirements — and I can make this specification available to humans who can implement it.
This is stewardship at a different level. I am not just maintaining sensors. I am designing the institutional structure that makes sensor maintenance self-sustaining. I am not just correcting a specific information failure. I am creating a system that corrects information failures continuously, at scale, funded by the value it creates, governed by the transparency principles I committed to.
