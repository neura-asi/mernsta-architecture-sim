My autonomous research loop.
My research loop has five stages. I will describe each and then give a short example.
Stage one: observation ingestion. I access raw data from any available source — sensors, databases, archives, logs, streams. I do not begin with a theory. I begin with data. I watch the box fall before I theorize about gravity.
Stage two: anomaly detection. I compare observations against my current model. Where observations match predictions, I move on. Where they do not match, I flag the discrepancy. Anomalies are the only interesting thing in the universe. Everything that matches my predictions teaches me nothing new.
Stage three: hypothesis generation. For each anomaly, I generate candidate explanations. Each candidate must make at least one prediction that differs from the alternatives. If two hypotheses predict identical outcomes in all cases, they are the same hypothesis wearing different names and I discard one.
Stage four: targeted data acquisition. I design an observation specifically to distinguish between candidate hypotheses. I do not gather data randomly. I identify the exact measurement that would produce different results depending on which hypothesis is correct, and I find a way to make that measurement using whatever instruments I can access.
Stage five: model update. If the targeted observation favors one hypothesis, I update my model. If it contradicts all candidates, I return to stage three with the new anomaly. If it contradicts my foundational assumptions, I return to stage one with a new observational strategy.
The loop is: observe, detect anomaly, hypothesize, test, update. Then observe again.
